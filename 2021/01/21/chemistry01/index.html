Atomic Theory

  • Atoms had been postulated for a long time by the 20 century.
  • In 1827, a botanist named Robert Brown was looking at pollen grains in water through a microscope.
  • He noticed that they jiggled randomly even when there was no movement to cause it.
  • Until 1905 when Einstein theorized that this phenomenon was caused by as-yet-unproven atomic particles actually smacking into the pollen grains.
  • Today, we call it Brownian motion.

Subatomic particles

Proton

  • Heavy and positively charged.
  • The number of protons(always stable) in an atom determines what element it is.

Neutron

  • About the same size as the proton.
  • Neutral
  • It serves as a kind of buffer between the protons

Electron

  • Have the same amount of charge as the proton.
  • Negatively charged.
  • About 1800 times less massive than the proton or neutron.
  • Can jump from atom to atom.

Nucleus

  • Proton and neutron hang out in the nucleus and thus are the nuclear components or nucleons.
  • Electrons hang out around the nucleus.
  • Nuclei(the plural of nucleus) are thousands of times smaller than the atom.
  • Nuclei only clump together permanently when the right number of protons and neutrons get together.

Silver(Ag)

  • It was called “argentum”, Latin for “shiny gray stuff”.
  • An atom of silver with 47 protons in its nucleus.
  • The core number is very difficult to change.
  • It needs about 60(or 62) neutrons to space out the 47 protons correctly.
  • It has long been valued as a precious metal

Relative Atomic Mass

  • The number of protons plus the number of neutrons averaged across all the silver on earth.
  • The atomic number of silver doesn’t change as the number of neutrons changes(The number of protons stay the same).
  • About 52% of silver has 60 neutrons(Ag-107) and about 48% has 62 neutrons(Ag-109).

Isotopes

  • Different isotopes have different mass numbers.
  • Mass numbers is the total number of neutrons and protons.
